# Hong Kong Profits Tax — Reference Pack v1 (2026-06)

This pack is canonical reference material for Hong Kong profits tax as
it applies to **AMZ Expert Global Limited**, a Hong Kong-incorporated
private company. It covers the territorial source principle, the
two-tier rate, the offshore claim, filing cadence, common foot-guns,
and audit triggers. It does NOT cover salaries tax (individuals),
property tax, or stamp duty.

This is reference material, not advice. Always confirm with a Hong
Kong tax practitioner before acting on it. Hong Kong tax law evolves
through Inland Revenue Department (IRD) practice notes; pack version
is v1 dated 2026-06.

## Territorial Source Principle

Hong Kong is a **territorial-source tax jurisdiction**. Hong Kong
profits tax is imposed only on profits **arising in or derived from
Hong Kong**. Profits derived from sources outside Hong Kong are
generally not taxable in Hong Kong, regardless of where the company
is incorporated or where it is managed.

This is the single most important principle for any HK-incorporated
trading or services company. It distinguishes Hong Kong from most
other developed jurisdictions, which tax their residents on worldwide
income. A Hong Kong company can be entirely tax-exempt in Hong Kong if
all its profits are derived from operations carried out outside Hong
Kong — even when the profits are remitted to Hong Kong bank accounts.

**The "operations test."** The leading authority is *CIR v Hang Seng
Bank Ltd* (1991) and *CIR v HK-TVB International* (1992), which
established that the source of profits is determined by identifying
what the taxpayer has done to earn the profit in question, and where
those operations took place. Where the profit-generating activities
took place determines source.

**For trading profits.** The IRD's general practice is to look at the
place where the **sale and purchase contracts** were effected. If
both buy and sell contracts were negotiated, concluded, and executed
outside Hong Kong, the profits are generally treated as offshore-sourced.
If either side of the transaction was substantially carried out in
Hong Kong, profits are typically considered Hong Kong-sourced.

**For services profits.** Source follows where the services are
**performed**. If services are performed entirely outside Hong Kong,
profits are generally offshore. Performance in Hong Kong taints
sourcing toward onshore.

**For interest, royalties, and other passive income.** Source is
determined by where the funds were provided or where the intellectual
property was used. Special rules apply.

**Recent IRD guidance.** Departmental Interpretation and Practice
Notes (DIPN) — particularly DIPN 21 (Trading Profits) and DIPN 22
(Service Companies) — set out the IRD's current view. The IRD has
become more rigorous since 2018 about requiring contemporaneous
documentation of the operations test, not just a year-end
characterization.

Always confirm source determinations with a Hong Kong tax practitioner
before claiming offshore treatment. The IRD will challenge
insufficiently-documented offshore claims, and the burden of proof
sits with the taxpayer.

## Two-Tier Profits Tax Rate

Hong Kong's profits tax rates depend on (1) whether the entity is a
corporation or unincorporated business and (2) whether the two-tier
regime applies.

**Standard rates (without the two-tier election).**
- Corporations: **16.5%** flat on assessable profits.
- Unincorporated businesses (sole proprietors, partnerships): 15% flat.

**Two-tier regime (introduced in 2018).**
- Corporations: **8.25%** on the first **HK$2 million** of assessable
  profits per year; **16.5%** on the remainder.
- Unincorporated businesses: 7.5% on the first HK$2M; 15% on the
  remainder.

**The two-tier restriction for connected entities.** Only **one
entity** per group of connected entities is allowed to enjoy the
two-tier rate in any year of assessment. "Connected entities" is
defined broadly and includes entities under common control,
subsidiaries, sister companies, and entities meeting certain
beneficial-ownership thresholds.

For a corporate group with multiple HK entities, the group must
**nominate** which entity enjoys the two-tier rate. The nomination is
made on the profits tax return of the nominated entity. Other group
entities pay the flat 16.5% on all profits with no two-tier
benefit.

**For AMZ Expert Global Limited.** As a single HK-incorporated
company without HK subsidiaries, the two-tier rate is available
without group-nomination concerns. The tax saving from two-tier vs
flat is HK$2M × (16.5% − 8.25%) = HK$165,000 per year at maximum.

**Effective tax rates.** With the two-tier rate, a corporation
earning HK$2M pays HK$165,000 (8.25% effective). At HK$10M, the
blended rate is around 14.85%. Above HK$50M, the rate approaches
the flat 16.5%.

Always confirm two-tier eligibility with a tax practitioner before
relying on it. If you add an HK subsidiary or join a group structure,
the nomination requirement applies.

## Offshore Profits Claim

The offshore profits claim is the practical mechanism by which a Hong
Kong company asserts that some or all of its profits are not subject
to Hong Kong profits tax under the territorial source principle. This
is one of the highest-value tax planning opportunities available to
HK-incorporated trading and services companies — and one of the most
scrutinized.

**How the claim is made.** A claim is asserted on the Profits Tax
Return (BIR51 or BIR52) by computing the assessable profits as if
some portion were offshore. The taxpayer keeps detailed working
papers supporting the claim. The IRD does NOT pre-approve offshore
claims; they are made on the return and the IRD may issue queries or
field-audit selected claims.

**Documentation required.** The standard expectation is that the
taxpayer maintains contemporaneous documentation showing:

- Where customers and suppliers are located.
- Where sale and purchase contracts were negotiated and concluded
  (correspondence trail, dates, locations of meetings).
- Where employees and key decision-makers were physically located
  when the relevant transactions were effected.
- Where the goods themselves moved (for trading) or where services
  were rendered (for services).
- Where supporting administrative functions occurred (back-office,
  bookkeeping, banking).

Travel records, email metadata, contracts, board minutes, and
correspondence records are all relied upon. The IRD has the power to
require production of these records and to question employees.

**Common offshore-claim patterns that succeed.**
- HK trading company sourcing from China, selling to non-Hong Kong
  customers, with all negotiation and contract-conclusion done by
  staff located outside Hong Kong (often in China or Singapore).
- HK services company providing services entirely to non-Hong Kong
  customers, with the services performed by staff located outside
  Hong Kong.
- HK company holding investments in non-HK companies, with the
  investment management decisions made outside Hong Kong.

**Common offshore-claim patterns that fail.**
- HK trading company with HK-based staff negotiating with HK-based
  customers or suppliers — even if goods physically move offshore.
- HK services company billing offshore but with services actually
  performed by HK-resident principals.
- Insufficient documentation: only year-end assertion, no
  contemporaneous proof of where operations occurred.

**The advance-ruling option.** A taxpayer may apply to the IRD for an
**advance ruling** (under section 88A of the Inland Revenue Ordinance)
on the source of specific profits. Fees apply (currently HK$45,000+
for source rulings). The ruling binds the IRD for the specific facts
ruled upon. This is useful for large or recurring transactions where
certainty matters more than the fee.

**FATCA / OECD CRS impact.** Offshore-source profits remain bank-
account-reported under FATCA and CRS regimes. The offshore claim
affects HK tax liability, not international transparency. Companies
should expect their banks to share account information with the
account-holder's tax residence jurisdiction.

Always document offshore claims contemporaneously, not retroactively.
The IRD's audit standards have tightened post-2018, and a successful
offshore claim depends entirely on the quality of the documentation
trail.

## Filing Calendar

The Hong Kong profits tax cycle runs around the company's accounting
year-end, with the Profits Tax Return (BIR51 for corporations) issued
by the IRD typically in April each year.

**Standard filing dates by year-end.**
- Accounting year-end 31 March (most common for HK corporations):
  - Return issued: April–May of the year of assessment.
  - Return due: 15 November of the year of assessment (extended
    deadline via tax representative; original due date is 1 May or
    similar based on the return).
- Accounting year-end 31 December:
  - Return issued: April–May.
  - Return due: 15 August of the year of assessment (extended via
    representative).
- Other year-ends: deadlines vary; the IRD's batch-filing system uses
  a code (D-code, M-code, N-code) based on year-end.

**Tax representative extensions.** Filing through a registered tax
representative (a CPA firm or qualified tax agent) entitles the
taxpayer to standard batch extensions. For 31 March year-ends, the
extended deadline is typically **15 November**. Without a
representative, the return is due by the date stated on the return
(generally 1 May).

**Provisional tax.** Once the IRD assesses the prior year's profits
tax, it issues a notice of assessment with both the final tax for
the prior year and **provisional tax** for the current year. The
provisional tax is set at 100% of the prior year's tax (or
sometimes a higher estimated figure). It is typically payable in
two installments:

- First installment: **75% of provisional tax** — payable by January
  or as specified.
- Second installment: **25% of provisional tax** — payable by April
  or as specified.

The provisional tax is credited against the actual final tax for the
current year when next year's assessment is issued.

**Holdover / objection to provisional tax.** A taxpayer can apply to
"hold over" provisional tax (defer payment) if they expect their
current-year profits to be substantially lower than the prior year.
The application must be made at least 28 days before the due date.

**Penalties for late filing.** Failure to file the Profits Tax Return
by the due date can trigger penalties under section 80(2) of the
Inland Revenue Ordinance — typically HK$10,000 first offense plus
treble the tax undercharged (additional tax). The IRD also has the
power to estimate assessments when returns are not filed and to
prosecute persistent non-compliance.

**Penalties for late payment.** Late payment of tax incurs a 5%
surcharge after 6 months past the due date, plus an additional 10%
surcharge after another 6 months.

Always confirm exact due dates with a Hong Kong tax representative —
batch-extension schedules can shift year to year, and IRD policy
notices update the timing.

## Documentation and Record-Keeping

Hong Kong's Inland Revenue Ordinance (IRO) section 51C requires
businesses to keep records sufficient to enable assessable profits
to be readily ascertained. The standard minimum is **7 years** from
the date of the transaction.

**Required records.** The IRO and IRD practice notes specify the
following minimum categories:

- Books of account showing all receipts and payments.
- Ledgers, journals, and supporting vouchers.
- Bank statements for all business accounts.
- Stock records (if applicable to the business).
- Contracts, invoices, receipts, and supporting commercial
  documents.
- Records substantiating any claims, including offshore-source claims.

**Penalties for inadequate records.** Failure to maintain proper
records is itself an offense under section 80(1A) of the IRO, with
penalties up to HK$100,000 plus prosecution risk. More commonly,
inadequate records lead the IRD to make estimated assessments based
on its own analysis (typically less favorable to the taxpayer than
the actual figures would be).

**Electronic records.** Electronic records are permitted but must be
accessible to the IRD on request and produced in a form the IRD can
read. Cloud-stored records are acceptable as long as access can be
provided.

**Offshore-claim records.** Records supporting offshore claims should
include contemporaneous evidence (emails, calendars, travel records,
contracts with signing-location notations, board minutes showing
where decisions were made). Retroactive documentation prepared at
year-end is treated by the IRD as low-credibility.

**Audit window.** The IRD can generally raise additional assessments
within 6 years of the year of assessment under section 60 of the
IRO. In cases of fraud or willful evasion, this window extends to
unlimited time.

Always maintain records in real time, not at year-end. The IRD's
records review can stretch back the full 6-year window, and gaps
in documentation create direct tax exposure.

## Common Foot-Guns

This section summarizes the most common errors and oversight patterns
that affect Hong Kong companies, particularly trading and services
SMEs.

**Failure to claim offshore source.** A surprising number of
Hong Kong companies pay HK profits tax on profits that would qualify
as offshore-sourced if properly documented and claimed. The default
position on the BIR51 is full HK-source treatment; the offshore claim
must be affirmatively asserted. Without the claim, no IRD official
will volunteer the benefit.

**Failure to document offshore source contemporaneously.** Companies
that wait until year-end to assemble offshore documentation
frequently lose the claim under IRD audit. The IRD evaluates
contemporaneous records (emails, contracts, travel records) much
more favorably than retroactive reconstructions.

**Two-tier rate confusion in group structures.** Multi-entity groups
sometimes incorrectly claim the two-tier rate on multiple entities.
Only one entity per group qualifies; failure to nominate correctly
triggers IRD adjustments and potentially penalties.

**Late filing without tax representative engagement.** Companies that
miss the standard deadline because they didn't engage a tax
representative lose the batch-extension benefit and become liable
for late-filing penalties. Engaging a CPA or qualified tax agent
months before the deadline is the standard practice.

**Failure to pay provisional tax.** Provisional tax is real, due-date
cash. Treating it as optional triggers 5% then 15% surcharges, plus
collection action by the IRD.

**Incorrect treatment of bad debts and inventory.** Bad debt
deductions and inventory write-downs have specific HK rules. Common
errors include claiming deductions before the debt has crystallized
or writing down inventory below cost without supporting evidence.

**Non-arm's-length related-party transactions.** Hong Kong's transfer
pricing rules require related-party transactions to be at arm's
length and supported by contemporaneous documentation when the safe-
harbor thresholds are exceeded. See the separate transfer-pricing
pack for details.

**Missed treaty benefits.** Hong Kong has comprehensive double-
taxation arrangements (CDTAs) with many jurisdictions (China,
Singapore, UK, many others — but **not the United States**). Failure
to claim treaty benefits on cross-border income can result in
double taxation.

Always engage a qualified Hong Kong tax practitioner annually before
filing. The cost of an annual review is trivial compared to the
penalties and unclaimed benefits from any of the above.

## Audit and IRD Field Audit Patterns

The Inland Revenue Department conducts field audits and investigations
selectively. Understanding what attracts attention is the cheapest
form of risk management.

**Profile of typical audit triggers.**

- Large offshore-source claims, especially first-time or rapidly-
  growing ones, frequently attract follow-up queries.
- Substantial related-party transactions (intercompany loans,
  services, IP licensing) without contemporaneous TP documentation.
- Sharp changes in profitability year-over-year without obvious
  business explanation.
- Year-end transactions or accruals that shift income or expenses
  across years.
- Discrepancies between business activity and reported profits (e.g.
  high turnover with low margin in industries where standard margins
  are higher).
- Public information mismatches (e.g. press coverage of a sale not
  reflected in the return).

**Audit process.** A typical IRD field audit starts with a written
request for information and documents under section 51(4) or
section 51A of the IRO. Failure to respond triggers escalation
including potential prosecution. The IRD may interview key
employees and request access to electronic records.

**Audit outcomes.** Possible outcomes include:

- No adjustment (clean audit).
- Adjustment to taxable profits with additional tax assessed.
- Additional tax penalty (section 82A) — up to treble the
  undercharged tax for negligence or fraud.
- Prosecution under section 80 or 82 of the IRO for willful evasion,
  carrying potential imprisonment and substantial fines.

**Penalty mitigation.** Voluntary disclosure of errors before IRD
discovery generally reduces penalties substantially. The IRD operates
a voluntary disclosure programme with reduced penalties for taxpayers
who come forward proactively about prior-year errors.

**The Comprehensive Avoidance of Double Taxation Agreement
(CDTA) framework.** Hong Kong has a growing network of double-taxation
treaties. The principal mechanism for resolving disputes about source
or characterization is the Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) under the
relevant CDTA. MAP is slow but effective for high-stakes cases.

Always respond promptly and through your tax representative when the
IRD opens a query. Delayed or incomplete responses materially
worsen audit outcomes, even when the underlying position is correct.

## Tax Incentives and Deductions

Hong Kong's tax incentive framework is narrow compared to many
jurisdictions but contains several meaningful provisions worth knowing.

**Research and development (R&D) super-deduction.** Type B R&D
expenditure (qualifying in-house R&D conducted in Hong Kong) qualifies
for a **300% deduction** on the first HK$2 million of qualifying
expenditure per year and **200% deduction** on the excess.
Type A R&D (outsourced or expenditure not meeting Type B criteria)
qualifies for a standard 100% deduction.

Eligibility requires that the R&D be conducted in Hong Kong or
contracted to a designated local research institution. Documentation
of the activities, costs, and Hong Kong nexus is mandatory.

**Capital allowances.** Plant and machinery: an initial allowance of
**60%** in the year of acquisition plus an annual allowance of
**10%, 20%, or 30%** on the reducing-balance basis depending on the
category. Industrial and commercial buildings: 4% per year.

Computer hardware and software: typically eligible for an immediate
100% write-off in the year of acquisition.

**Patents, copyrights, and registered designs.** Purchase costs are
generally deductible over five years on a straight-line basis (20%
per year) under section 16E of the IRO.

**Charitable donations.** Donations to approved Hong Kong charities
are deductible up to **35% of assessable profits** per year.

**Loss carry-forward.** Trading losses can be carried forward
indefinitely against future profits, **provided there is no change
of shareholding** combined with a change of business activity. Sale
of an HK loss company to a buyer who changes its activities risks
losing the accumulated losses under anti-avoidance rules.

**Family office concessions and aircraft/ship leasing.** Special
tax regimes apply to qualifying family office structures and
qualifying ship/aircraft leasing — niche but valuable where
applicable.

**Capital vs revenue distinction.** Hong Kong distinguishes
sharply between capital expenditure (not deductible, but eligible
for capital allowances) and revenue expenditure (deductible).
Common borderline cases include extensive repairs vs improvements
and software development vs purchase.

Always confirm eligibility for incentives with a tax practitioner.
The R&D super-deduction in particular has rigorous documentation
requirements that, if missed, lose the benefit entirely.

## Cross-Border and Related-Party Considerations

Hong Kong's transfer pricing regime and treaty network apply
extensively to companies engaged in international business.

**Transfer pricing rules.** Hong Kong introduced statutory transfer
pricing rules in 2018 (sections 50AAF–50AAS of the IRO). The rules
require related-party transactions to be at arm's length and require
contemporaneous documentation when certain thresholds are exceeded.
A separate transfer-pricing pack covers these rules in depth.

**Country-by-country reporting (CbCR).** Multinational groups with
consolidated annual revenue **above EUR 750 million** (or HK
equivalent threshold) must file a CbCR. This is unlikely to apply to
AMZ Expert Global at current scale, but it's worth knowing the
threshold exists.

**Base erosion and profit shifting (BEPS) Pillar Two.** Hong Kong is
implementing the OECD Pillar Two minimum 15% global tax regime,
which applies to multinational enterprise groups with consolidated
revenue above EUR 750 million. This is not applicable to AMZ Expert
Global at current scale and is unlikely to be unless the group grows
substantially.

**Double-taxation arrangements.** Hong Kong has CDTAs with many major
jurisdictions including China, Singapore, the UK, Japan, the
Netherlands, France, Spain, and Canada. **There is no CDTA between
Hong Kong and the United States.** This is the single most consequential
treaty gap for AMZ Expert Global: payments between AMZ Expert Global
(HK) and Specific Edge Outsourcing LLC (US) cannot benefit from
treaty rate reductions on either side.

**Withholding tax in Hong Kong.** Hong Kong generally does not impose
withholding tax on dividends, interest, or services paid to non-HK
persons. Royalties paid to non-HK persons are subject to a deemed
profits regime resulting in an effective rate of typically 4.95%
(corporate non-resident at 30% of gross × 16.5%). This is one of the
most attractive features of Hong Kong as a holding jurisdiction.

**Permanent establishment risk.** A non-HK company with employees or
a fixed place of business in Hong Kong risks creating a Hong Kong
permanent establishment, triggering HK profits tax on the PE's
profits. This rarely affects HK-incorporated companies but is a
consideration for foreign owners contemplating using HK staff.

Always confirm cross-border tax positions with both a Hong Kong tax
practitioner and a tax practitioner in the counterparty jurisdiction.
Cross-border tax outcomes depend on both ends of the transaction.
