# Transfer Pricing HK ↔ US — Reference Pack v1 (2026-06)

This pack covers transfer pricing rules as they apply to intercompany
transactions between **AMZ Expert Global Limited** (Hong Kong parent)
and **Specific Edge Outsourcing LLC** (US subsidiary). It is the most
consequential cross-entity area for the group because no US-Hong Kong
income tax treaty exists, making both sides of every intercompany
charge independently scrutinizable by both tax authorities.

This is reference material, not advice. Always confirm intercompany
pricing arrangements with both a Hong Kong tax practitioner and a US
CPA before implementation. Transfer pricing is fact-intensive and
audit outcomes turn on documentation. Pack version is v1 dated 2026-06.

## The Arm's-Length Principle

The foundational rule of transfer pricing is the **arm's-length
principle**: transactions between related parties should be priced
as if the parties were independent enterprises dealing at arm's
length. This principle is codified in:

- **Hong Kong**: Inland Revenue Ordinance sections 50AAF–50AAS,
  introduced in 2018 to align with OECD BEPS Action 8–10
  recommendations.
- **United States**: Internal Revenue Code section 482, which
  empowers the IRS to allocate income and deductions between related
  parties to "clearly reflect income" — predating the OECD framework
  but operationally similar.

The OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines (most recently 2022 edition) are
the primary international reference. Both Hong Kong and the United
States look to OECD methodology, though each maintains its own
statutory framework and administrative practice.

**Practical implication.** Every charge between AMZ Expert Global and
Specific Edge Outsourcing — services rendered, royalties for IP,
interest on loans, sales of goods, management fees, cost
reimbursements — must be supportable as a price an unrelated party
would have charged for the same transaction under the same
circumstances. If the IRD or the IRS determines the pricing is not
at arm's length, the authority can adjust the income and impose tax,
penalties, and interest.

**Why both authorities scrutinize.** When the US and HK have no
income tax treaty, a transfer-pricing adjustment by one authority is
not automatically offset by the other. If the IRS reduces US LLC's
deductible expenses for an over-charged management fee from HK, the
HK side does not automatically reduce its taxable income to match.
The group can suffer economic double taxation. With treaty countries,
the Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP) provides a remedy; for HK-US
transactions, no MAP relief is available.

**Documentation as the audit foundation.** Both authorities focus
heavily on documentation. A well-documented intercompany
arrangement supported by contemporaneous benchmarking has dramatically
lower audit risk than an undocumented arrangement, even when the
underlying economics are identical.

Always engage transfer-pricing specialists for material intercompany
arrangements between AMZ Expert Global and Specific Edge Outsourcing.
The HK-US treaty gap means errors are not recoverable through
authority-to-authority negotiation.

## The Five OECD Transfer Pricing Methods

OECD methodology offers five methods for determining arm's-length
pricing. Both Hong Kong and the US accept all five, with different
methods being preferred for different transaction types.

**Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP).** The price of an identical or
nearly-identical transaction between unrelated parties is used as
the benchmark. CUP is the most direct method and is preferred when
applicable, but truly comparable transactions are rare in practice.
Used for commodity sales, well-defined services with public price
data, and certain financial transactions where market rates are
observable.

**Resale Price Method (RPM).** Used primarily for distribution
arrangements. The resale price to an unrelated party is reduced by
an appropriate gross margin (the "resale price margin") that would
compensate the reseller for performing distribution functions. The
remainder is the arm's-length cost to the related-party supplier.

**Cost Plus Method.** Used for routine services and manufacturing. The
costs incurred by the supplier are marked up by an arm's-length
margin (typically 5–10% for routine services, higher for complex
ones). For Specific Edge Outsourcing as a US service provider to
the HK parent, cost-plus is often the natural method.

**Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM).** The most commonly applied
method in practice. TNMM examines the net profit margin earned by the
tested party (the simpler entity in the transaction) and compares it
to net margins earned by comparable independent enterprises. Margins
are typically measured against an appropriate base such as sales,
costs, or assets.

**Profit Split Method.** Used when both parties make non-routine
contributions (e.g. both contribute valuable IP). The combined
profits from the transaction are split between the parties based on
their relative contributions, typically measured by some economic
metric.

**Method selection.** OECD guidance prefers traditional transaction
methods (CUP, RPM, Cost Plus) where reliable comparables exist, and
permits transactional profit methods (TNMM, Profit Split) where
traditional methods are unreliable. Both authorities accept the "best
method" principle: use whichever method provides the most reliable
arm's-length result given available data.

**For service charges between SEO and AMZG.** Cost-plus is typically
appropriate where SEO performs routine support services for AMZG.
Markups in the 5–10% range are commonly accepted for routine,
non-strategic functions.

**For intercompany loans.** CUP is the natural method — interest
rates on comparable arm's-length loans between unrelated parties.
The base rate plus a credit-risk spread reflecting the borrower's
standalone creditworthiness.

**For royalties.** CUP if comparable license arrangements exist;
otherwise Profit Split when the IP is jointly developed, or Cost
Plus on the development costs when the IP is owned by one entity
and contracted to the other.

Always document the method selection rationale. Selecting an
inappropriate method without explanation is a frequent audit
finding.

## Hong Kong Transfer Pricing Requirements

Hong Kong's statutory transfer pricing regime took effect on
13 July 2018 with the enactment of the Inland Revenue (Amendment)
(No. 6) Ordinance 2018, adding sections 50AAF–50AAS to the IRO.

**Rule 1 — the arm's-length principle.** Hong Kong taxpayers must
price intra-group transactions at arm's length. Where the actual
pricing differs from arm's length and Hong Kong tax is reduced as
a result, the IRD may adjust assessable profits.

**Rule 2 — documentation requirements (Master File and Local File).**
Hong Kong adopted OECD's three-tier documentation structure:

- **Country-by-Country Report (CbCR)** — for ultimate parent entities
  of multinational groups with consolidated revenue **above
  HK\$6.8 billion** (approximately EUR 750 million). Not applicable
  to AMZ Expert Global at current scale.
- **Master File** — high-level information about the multinational
  group. Required when the group exceeds the size thresholds
  triggering CbCR, OR when local exemption conditions are not met.
- **Local File** — detailed information about the local entity's
  intercompany transactions.

**Safe-harbor thresholds for Master/Local File exemption.** A Hong
Kong entity is exempt from preparing Master File and Local File for
a year of assessment if it meets at least one of the following:

- The entity's annual revenue does not exceed **HK\$400 million**, AND
- The entity's total assets do not exceed **HK\$300 million**, AND
- The entity's average number of employees does not exceed **100**.

OR

- Total controlled transactions during the year do not exceed
  specified thresholds (currently HK\$220M for property, HK\$110M for
  financial, HK\$44M for services, HK\$44M for IP).

For AMZ Expert Global, whether these safe harbors apply depends on
actual figures. If the company is below the thresholds, formal
Master/Local Files are not required — but the substantive arm's-
length pricing rule still applies, and the IRD can still query
intercompany transactions.

**Master File contents (when required).** Group structure, lines of
business, intangibles, intercompany financial activities, group
financial and tax positions.

**Local File contents (when required).** Description of the local
entity, controlled transactions, financial information, related-party
identification, transfer pricing analysis with benchmarking, method
selection rationale.

**Filing timing.** Master File and Local File must be prepared by the
return-filing deadline for the year and retained for **at least
7 years**. They are not filed with the return but must be produced
to the IRD on request, typically within 30 days.

**Penalties.** Failure to comply with documentation requirements
carries penalties under section 80(1B) of the IRO — up to HK\$50,000
per offense. Substantive transfer-pricing adjustments trigger
additional tax penalties under section 82A — up to treble the tax
undercharged.

**Advance Pricing Arrangements (APAs).** The IRD operates an APA
programme allowing taxpayers to negotiate transfer-pricing methods
in advance for a defined period (typically 3–5 years). Three types:
unilateral (HK only), bilateral (HK + treaty partner), and multilateral.
Useful for large or recurring transactions where certainty justifies
the upfront effort.

Always assess whether Master/Local File requirements apply
year-by-year. The thresholds are based on actual figures for the
year, not budgeted or prior-year figures.

## United States Transfer Pricing Requirements

US transfer pricing is administered under IRC section 482 and the
extensive Treasury regulations. The US has the longest-running and
most detailed transfer pricing regime in the world.

**Section 482 — the arm's-length standard.** Permits the IRS to
allocate income, deductions, credits, or allowances among controlled
taxpayers to clearly reflect income. The "best method" rule applies:
select the method providing the most reliable measure of arm's-length
result.

**Documentation requirements.** US documentation rules differ from
HK and OECD:

- **Contemporaneous documentation** must be prepared by the tax
  return due date (including extensions) for transactions to qualify
  for penalty protection under section 6662.
- Documentation must include a principal document covering the
  taxpayer's business, organizational structure, controlled
  transactions, method selection, comparables, and conclusions.
- Documentation must be in English.
- Documentation must be provided to the IRS within **30 days** of a
  request.

**Penalty regime.** Substantial valuation misstatement penalties
under section 6662(e):

- **20% penalty** if the transfer price is 200% or more of the correct
  arm's-length price (or 50% or less), OR if the adjustment exceeds
  the lesser of \$5 million or 10% of gross receipts.
- **40% penalty** if the transfer price is 400% or more of the correct
  arm's-length price (or 25% or less), OR if the adjustment exceeds
  the lesser of \$20 million or 20% of gross receipts.

**Penalty protection.** The 20%/40% penalties do not apply if the
taxpayer maintained contemporaneous documentation supporting a
reasonable selection and application of the section 482 methods.
This is the primary reason US taxpayers prepare extensive
contemporaneous TP documentation even when no formal filing is
required.

**Form 5472 cross-reference.** The Form 5472 filed by the US LLC
(see the US LLC tax pack) reports the existence of related-party
transactions. The IRS uses Form 5472 to identify transactions
warranting further scrutiny under section 482.

**Cost Sharing Arrangements (CSAs).** Special rules under
Treas. Reg. 1.482-7 for arrangements where related parties share
the costs and risks of developing intangibles. CSAs are powerful
planning tools but heavily regulated; the rules require specific
documentation, periodic adjustments, and "platform contribution
transactions" for pre-existing IP. Likely not applicable to AMZ
Expert Global / SEO at current scale.

**Advance Pricing Agreements (APAs).** The US operates an APA
program through the IRS Advance Pricing and Mutual Agreement Program
(APMA). Bilateral APAs with treaty countries are available; for
non-treaty jurisdictions (Hong Kong), only unilateral APAs are
available.

**IRS audit focus.** The IRS audits transfer pricing for foreign-owned
US subsidiaries with heightened scrutiny when:

- Intercompany service charges are large relative to the US entity's
  income.
- IP is owned offshore and licensed to the US entity.
- Loan structures result in significant interest deductions for the
  US entity.
- The foreign parent is in a low-tax jurisdiction (Hong Kong fits
  this profile).

Always prepare US contemporaneous documentation by the US return
due date. Late documentation does not provide penalty protection.

## Common HK ↔ US Intercompany Structures

This section describes typical intercompany arrangements between an
HK parent and a US subsidiary, their transfer-pricing implications,
and the documentation expected.

**Pattern 1: US sub provides services to HK parent.** SEO performs
services (sales, customer support, operations, US-market business
development) for AMZG's benefit. AMZG pays SEO a service fee.

- Method: cost-plus (typically 5–10% markup on direct + allocated
  costs).
- Documentation: cost allocation methodology, time tracking, scope
  of services, benchmark study of comparable independent service
  providers.
- HK side: the payment is a deductible expense for AMZG. Properly
  documented, the cost is allowed.
- US side: the markup is taxable income for SEO; the underlying
  costs are deductible. Arm's-length markup avoids IRS section 482
  adjustment.

**Pattern 2: HK parent provides services to US sub.** AMZG performs
services (strategic management, IP licensing, group treasury) for
SEO's benefit. SEO pays AMZG a service or management fee.

- Method: cost-plus or CUP depending on the nature of services.
- Documentation: arm's-length determination of the fee, benefit
  test (the US sub must receive a real economic benefit), service
  scope, who performs the services.
- US side: the payment is generally deductible by SEO — but the IRS
  scrutinizes management fees from foreign parents heavily. Inadequate
  documentation results in disallowance.
- HK side: AMGZ's receipt is potentially HK-source income subject
  to HK profits tax (or potentially offshore if the services are
  performed entirely outside HK by non-HK staff — but this requires
  documentation supporting the offshore claim).

**Pattern 3: Intercompany loan.** AMZG lends funds to SEO for working
capital; SEO pays interest.

- Method: CUP — comparable interest rates on arm's-length loans
  with similar credit risk, currency, tenor.
- Documentation: loan agreement, credit analysis of SEO's standalone
  creditworthiness, benchmark interest rate.
- US side: the interest is generally deductible by SEO, subject to
  the earnings-stripping rules of IRC section 163(j) (limit on
  interest deductions to 30% of adjusted taxable income).
- US withholding: interest paid to AMZG is generally US-source FDAP
  income subject to 30% US withholding tax (no treaty rate available
  for HK).
- HK side: AMZG's receipt is income; offshore-source claim possible
  if loan management activities occurred outside HK.

**Pattern 4: Royalty for IP.** AMZG owns IP (brand, software,
methodology); licenses to SEO; SEO pays royalty.

- Method: CUP if comparable licenses exist; Profit Split if jointly
  developed.
- Documentation: license agreement, IP ownership documentation,
  benefit to SEO, benchmark royalty study.
- US side: royalty deductible by SEO; withholding tax at 30% on
  gross royalty paid to HK (no treaty reduction). This makes IP
  licensing from HK to US relatively unattractive economically.
- HK side: AMZG's receipt is subject to the HK deemed-profits regime
  for royalties received from HK-source IP use, resulting in an
  effective HK tax rate of ~4.95% on gross royalty.

**Pattern 5: Sale of services from US to non-US customers (third
parties).** SEO performs services for non-US customers; payments
flow to SEO; SEO may remit profits to AMZG.

- Not directly a transfer-pricing issue (the customer is unrelated).
- But the question of who is the principal vs the agent in the
  customer relationship determines whether revenue accrues to SEO
  (with US tax) or to AMZG (with HK tax). The intercompany
  arrangement between SEO and AMZG must reflect economic reality.

Always design intercompany arrangements before, not after, the
transactions occur. Retroactive structuring is heavily disfavored
by both authorities.

## Documentation Best Practices

Strong documentation is the single most cost-effective transfer-
pricing risk control. The cost of preparing contemporaneous
documentation is dramatically lower than defending an unsupported
position in audit.

**Minimum documentation set for each material intercompany
transaction:**

- **Written intercompany agreement.** Dated before or contemporaneously
  with the transaction. Specifies scope, deliverables, pricing
  methodology, term, governing law, dispute resolution.
- **Functional analysis.** Identifies which entity performs which
  functions, owns which assets, and bears which risks. The entity
  with greater functions/assets/risks is generally entitled to
  greater profit.
- **Method selection rationale.** Explains why the selected method is
  appropriate and why other methods were rejected.
- **Benchmark study.** Identifies comparable arm's-length transactions
  or comparable independent enterprises. Sources: commercial
  databases (Bloomberg, Bureau van Dijk), public filings, industry
  reports.
- **Financial analysis.** Demonstrates that the actual pricing falls
  within the arm's-length range derived from the benchmarks.
- **Annual update.** Documentation should be reviewed and updated
  each year — even if the underlying transactions are unchanged,
  changes in market conditions, currency rates, or benchmarks may
  require refreshed pricing.

**Contemporaneous means contemporaneous.** Documentation prepared
months or years after the transaction is materially less credible
in audit. Both authorities heavily discount late-prepared
documentation. Aim to have documentation in place by the time of
the first material intercompany transaction in a year and to refresh
it before each return-filing deadline.

**Both-sides view.** Documentation should anticipate scrutiny by
both the IRD and the IRS. A single set of documents that satisfies
both jurisdictions' substantive requirements is more efficient than
preparing separate jurisdiction-specific documents (though formal
filings may differ).

**Common documentation gaps that lose audits:**

- No written intercompany agreement.
- Agreement exists but is signed years after the transactions began.
- Functional analysis describes the legal structure but not the
  actual operational realities (employees, decisions, risks).
- Benchmark study uses outdated comparables.
- Method selection is asserted but not justified.
- Documentation is in a foreign language (specifically problematic
  for US — Treasury regs require English).
- No annual update or refresh.

Always treat documentation as a core operational task, not a year-end
afterthought. A 30-minute monthly review during the year is cheaper
than 50 hours of reconstruction during audit.

## Audit Risk and Penalty Exposure

Transfer pricing is one of the higher-risk audit areas for any
multinational structure. Understanding the patterns that attract
scrutiny is essential.

**Top IRD audit triggers for HK side:**

- Large or rapidly-growing related-party transactions, particularly
  service fees from HK to non-HK entities.
- Significant offshore-claim positions for HK source income that
  involves substantial intra-group flows.
- HK entities with very low profit margins relative to industry
  norms.
- Inconsistencies between intercompany agreements and operational
  reality.
- Failure to prepare Master/Local File when thresholds are exceeded.

**Top IRS audit triggers for US side:**

- Form 5472 disclosures showing large related-party transactions.
- US entities with very low margins or losses while the foreign
  parent has substantial income.
- Management fees paid to foreign parents in low-tax jurisdictions.
- Royalty payments to foreign parents where the IP development
  history involves the US entity.
- Loan structures that produce large interest deductions for the
  US entity.

**Penalty exposure summary.**

| Authority | Penalty for adjustment | Penalty for documentation gap |
|---|---|---|
| Hong Kong IRD | Up to treble undercharged tax (s.82A) | Up to HK\$50,000 (s.80(1B)) |
| US IRS | 20% or 40% of adjustment (s.6662) | Loss of penalty protection |

**The HK-US treaty gap and economic double taxation.** Because no US-
HK income tax treaty exists, a transfer-pricing adjustment by one
authority is not automatically offset by the other. If the IRS
disallows a US deduction for an over-paid management fee, the HK
side cannot claim a corresponding deduction or refund. The group
ends up paying tax on the same income in both jurisdictions.

This makes correct pricing from day one materially more important
for HK-US transactions than for transactions between treaty countries.

**Best protection.** Implement contemporaneous documentation,
maintain consistent intercompany agreements, refresh benchmarks
annually, and engage qualified transfer-pricing advisors for any
new material transaction type.

Always assume audit. The cost of preparing for audit upfront is
trivially small compared to the cost of unwinding an audit
adjustment after the fact, especially in non-treaty bilateral
contexts.

## Practical Implementation Roadmap

This section synthesizes the above into a concrete implementation
sequence for AMZ Expert Global and Specific Edge Outsourcing.

**Step 1 — inventory existing intercompany flows.** List every
recurring or material transaction between AMZG and SEO:

- Services in either direction.
- Loans or capital contributions.
- IP licensing or royalties.
- Cost reimbursements.
- Sales of goods (if applicable).

**Step 2 — for each flow, decide method and pricing.** For each item
in the inventory, decide the TP method (CUP, Cost Plus, etc.), the
arm's-length price, and the supporting rationale. Engage a TP
specialist for material items.

**Step 3 — implement written intercompany agreements.** For each
ongoing arrangement, prepare a written intercompany agreement signed
by authorized representatives of both entities. The agreement should
reference the pricing methodology and provide for periodic review.

**Step 4 — implement invoicing and settlement processes.** Each
intercompany charge should be invoiced contemporaneously (typically
monthly), with cash settlement within commercial terms (typically
30 days). Treating intercompany invoices as informal or settling them
only at year-end weakens the substantive arm's-length position.

**Step 5 — prepare or refresh transfer-pricing documentation.** Build
a TP file containing the functional analysis, method selection,
benchmark study, and financial analysis for each material transaction.
Refresh annually before the earlier of the HK or US return-filing
deadline.

**Step 6 — coordinate filings.** Ensure that:
- AMZG's HK return reflects the intercompany flows consistently.
- SEO's Form 5472 reports all reportable transactions with AMZG.
- Withholding tax obligations (US 30% withholding on interest or
  royalties paid to AMZG) are met and documented on Forms 1042 /
  1042-S.
- HK Master File / Local File requirements are met if applicable
  thresholds are exceeded.

**Step 7 — monitor for changes.** Any material change in the business
(new product line, new customers in different jurisdictions, change
in personnel) may shift the appropriate TP method or the arm's-length
range. Periodic review (annually at minimum) catches drift before it
becomes an audit issue.

**Common operational shortcuts to avoid.**
- Reimbursing costs with no markup (the IRS often imputes a markup,
  and inconsistencies attract scrutiny).
- Implementing arrangements without written agreements ("we'll paper
  it later").
- Using stale comparables (e.g. a 2018 benchmark study applied in
  2026 without refresh).
- Treating intercompany items differently for management accounting
  vs. tax filing.

Always engage qualified transfer-pricing specialists before
implementing significant intercompany arrangements. The cost of
specialist advice is materially lower than the cost of remediation
after the fact, especially given the HK-US treaty gap.
